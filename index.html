About

From Blantyre to
the frontier of climate finance

I'm a Level 5 Computing student at NACIT, Blantyre, building at the intersection of artificial intelligence, satellite remote sensing, and financial infrastructure for Africa's most underserved communities.

RaFund is the vehicle I founded to direct technology investment toward one of the continent's most persistent problems: the catastrophic gap between when a drought destroys a harvest and when a farmer receives financial relief. Traditionally that gap is 4–12 weeks. With parametric insurance, it is zero.

My flagship project — an AI-driven parametric insurance platform in active development with NICO General Insurance, Malawi's market leader — replaces manual farm inspection with satellite data from NASA and ESA, drone-based multispectral imaging, and IoT ground sensors. When a drought threshold is crossed, mobile money flows to the farmer automatically.

RafeTech is the software studio that builds and maintains the technology stack behind RaFund's investment thesis. Every system we build is open to partnership, licensing, and co-investment.

Parametric Insurance Platform
— NICO General Insurance

Drought Prediction Model
— ML Risk Engine

A scikit-learn classification and regression model trained on localized Malawian climate and crop phenology data. Produces per-plot drought risk scores that feed directly into parametric trigger thresholds. Tracked with MLflow and served via FastAPI. Directly addresses the 2016/17 Africa Risk Capacity miscalibration failure where model mismatch led to low, delayed payouts.

Python scikit-learn MLflow pandas FastAPI
◌ In Planning

Crop Health Vision API
— Drone Image Analysis

An OpenCV + YOLOv8 computer vision pipeline that processes multispectral drone imagery to detect nitrogen deficiencies, water stress, and early-stage pest infestations at centimetre resolution. Returns structured NDVI crop health reports that confirm or override satellite-level drought signals, minimizing basis risk.

Investment Thesis

Why RaFund

The structural case for parametric agricultural insurance in Malawi and the model for deploying capital that creates both financial return and measurable climate resilience impact.

01

The Infrastructure Gap is Now Closeable

Satellite resolution, drone costs, and mobile money penetration in Malawi have reached the threshold where smallholder parametric insurance is operationally viable. The barriers that existed five years ago — data scarcity, adjuster costs, unbanked distribution — are all solvable with technology available today.

02

In-Country Partnership De-Risks Distribution

NICO General Insurance holds a 35% market share, an AA claims-paying rating, and an existing agricultural portfolio covering sugarcane, tobacco, and cereals. RafeTech provides the technology layer; NICO provides the regulatory standing, client relationships, and capital. The distribution problem is already solved.

03

The Market is Expanding, Not Contracting

African agrifoodtech funding reached $192 million in 2024 — more than 600% above 2014 levels. Parametric insurance specifically is growing at 11% annually in Africa and the Middle East. We are entering a market at the right moment, with the right partner, in an underfunded geography.

04

Proprietary Data Becomes the Moat

Every season of drone and satellite data collected over Malawian fields becomes proprietary training data that improves model accuracy and cannot be replicated without the same field time. The platform compounds in value year over year. Early investors enter before that data moat is established.

05

Impact and Return Are Aligned, Not in Tension

Every payout that reaches a farmer before their crops die prevents a humanitarian outcome — and builds policyholder trust that drives renewal rates. The product is financially sound precisely because it pays claims quickly and accurately. Impact metrics and financial metrics point in the same direction.
